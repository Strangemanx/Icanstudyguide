Topic 01

Personal Income Tax — Sole Traders

The biggest single topic in ICAN Taxation. Computing adjusted profit and personal income tax for a sole proprietor was the full 30-mark compulsory question in Nov 2025.

1 Computing Adjusted Profit

Start with net profit per accounts, then add back disallowable expenses and deduct allowable items omitted. The golden rule: an expense is only deductible if it's wholly, reasonably, exclusively and necessarily (WREN) incurred for the business.

Adjusted Profit — Standard Format
Net profit per accounts X Add: Understated turnover/income omitted X Add: Income/recoveries not in accounts X Add: Disallowable (private/capital) expenses X ___ X Less: Allowable expenses omitted from accounts (X) ___ Adjusted profit X
⚠ Common disallowable items (Nov 2025 Q1): private portion of rent/rates, private entertainment, donations to non-approved bodies, capital expenditure disguised as revenue (e.g. equipment in "miscellaneous"), private medical expenses for non-dependents, interest on personal-use portion of a loan, capital repairs (reconstruction of premises), depreciation.

2 Worked Example — Nov 2025 Q1 (Oke & Sons Enterprises)

Adjustments Applied
Net profit per accounts: 10,410,000 Add: Understated turnover: 2,700,000 Add: Bad debt recovered (was a credit): 260,000 13,370,000 Add disallowable: Rent/rates (1/3 private use): 400,000 Entertainment (1/3 private birthday): 296,667 Miscellaneous (15% personal): 232,500 Donations (non-deductible by default): 750,000 Equipment bought, posted to misc.: 600,000 Reconstruction of entrance gate (capital): 1,600,000 Interest on personal-use loan: 160,000 Medical expenses (non-dependent, mum): 200,000 Depreciation (always added back): 2,640,000 6,879,167 20,249,167 Less: Allowable expenses omitted: (770,000) ADJUSTED PROFIT: 19,479,167
Why medical expenses for "his mum" were disallowed: Allowable medical relief only covers the taxpayer, spouse, and unmarried children under his care. A parent does not qualify as a dependent for this purpose under PITA.

3 Computing the Income Tax Liability

Full Tax Computation Sequence
Adjusted profit X Add: Balancing charge (restricted to capital allowance previously claimed) X ___ X Less: Capital allowances (b/f + current year) (X) ___ X Add: Other income (interest, rent, dividend - GROSS) X ___ X Less: Franked investment income (dividend, exempt)(X) ___ X Less: Tax-exempt items (pension, life assurance) (X) ___ Gross income X Less: Consolidated Relief Allowance (CRA) (X) ___ Chargeable income X CRA = Higher of (N200,000 OR 1% of gross income) + 20% of gross income
⚠ Grossing up "net" income: Interest and rent are often given net of withholding tax (10%). Gross up by dividing by (1 – tax rate), e.g. Net ÷ 0.9 for 10% WHT. This grossed-up WHT is then deducted as tax already paid against the final liability.

4 The Graduated Tax Table (PITA)

Band (₦)Rate
First 300,0007%
Next 300,00011%
Next 500,00015%
Next 500,00019%
Next 1,600,00021%
Above 3,200,00024%

Apply each band cumulatively to the chargeable income, then deduct withholding tax already suffered on interest/rent to get net tax payable.

5 Minimum Tax

Minimum tax applies when:

  • The taxpayer has no taxable income for the year (often due to heavy reliefs and deductions), OR
  • The actual tax computed using graduated rates is less than the minimum tax threshold
Exemption: Individuals earning the national minimum wage or less are exempt from all income tax. Since May 1, 2024, the national minimum wage is ₦70,000/month (₦840,000/year).

3 Instalment Payment of Tax — Section 77(5) Finance Act 2019

A company may elect to settle its tax liability by instalments, subject to conditions:

  • An initial payment of at least 50% of the tax liability must be made on or before the due date for filing
  • The balance must be settled within a period agreed with the tax authority — typically within 2 months
  • The company must have a good compliance history with the tax authority
  • The arrangement is at the discretion of the FIRS, not an automatic right

Capital allowances replace accounting depreciation for tax purposes. Get the initial and annual rates right — they're given in every exam's rate table.

Investment allowance: Up to August 31, 2023, an additional 10% investment allowance applied on qualifying plant expenditure. From September 1, 2023 (Finance Act 2023), this was reduced to NIL.

2 Balancing Charges and Allowances

When an asset is sold (disposed):

  • If sale proceeds > tax written down value → Balancing Charge (added back to assessable profit, but capped at total capital allowances previously claimed)
  • If sale proceeds < tax written down value → Balancing Allowance (further capital allowance granted)
🎯 Nov 2025 Q1 — Balancing Charge Cap

A qualifying capital asset was sold resulting in a balancing charge of ₦1,200,000. However, capital allowance already claimed on the asset was only ₦900,000. The balancing charge to tax is restricted to ₦900,000 — you can never be charged tax on more than the allowances you actually claimed.

Topic 04

Interest Deductibility & Thin Capitalisation Rules

Section 24 CITA and the 7th Schedule restrict excessive interest deductions on loans from foreign connected persons — tested in Nov 2025 Q2(b).

1 Why Thin Capitalisation Rules Exist

Multinational groups can shift profits out of Nigeria by having a Nigerian subsidiary borrow heavily from a foreign parent and pay large interest charges (which are tax-deductible) instead of receiving equity (dividends, which are not deductible). Thin capitalisation rules cap how much interest on related-party foreign debt can be deducted.

2 The Restriction Formula

Section 24 CITA / 7th Schedule (Finance Act)
Maximum deductible interest on related-party foreign debt = 30% of EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation and Amortisation) Where interest paid to a foreign connected person exceeds this cap, the EXCESS is disallowed for the current year, but can typically be carried forward and deducted in future years (subject to the same 30% EBITDA cap each year), normally up to 5 years. Interest paid to fund TAX-EXEMPT income is never deductible at all (matching principle — you cannot deduct costs of earning exempt income).

3 Tax Implication of Disallowed Excess Interest

  • The disallowed excess does NOT disappear permanently — it can be carried forward
  • It is deductible in subsequent years, subject again to the 30% EBITDA limit in that year
  • There's typically a time limit (e.g. 5 years) after which unutilised excess interest expires
  • This prevents permanent erosion of the tax base while still allowing genuine long-term financing costs to eventually be recognised

Topic 05

Value Added Tax (VAT)

Computing VAT payable, understanding zero-rated vs exempt supplies, and registration/deregistration rules — tested in Nov 2025 Q3.

1 The VAT Mechanism

Basic VAT Computation
VAT Payable = Output VAT – Input VAT Output VAT = VAT charged on TAXABLE sales (standard rate 7.5%) Input VAT = VAT paid on TAXABLE purchases/expenses used for the business Output VAT - Input VAT = Positive → Pay to FIRS = Negative → Refundable / carried forward

2 Items That Don't Generate Claimable Input VAT

Not all VAT paid is reclaimable. The VAT Act disallows input VAT on:

  • Overhead/admin items not directly tied to taxable supplies in some cases — but generally most genuine business inputs ARE allowable
  • VAT on items related to zero-rated or exempt supplies only needs careful apportionment
  • Sales returns reduce taxable turnover for output VAT computation
🎯 Nov 2025 Q3 — Ganzi Nigeria Limited VAT Computation

Key adjustments: exempt/zero-rated supplies (₦15m) must be EXCLUDED from output VAT base. Sales returns reduce taxable revenue. Purchases returned reduce input VAT claimable. All figures given VAT-inclusive must be grossed down by dividing by 1.075 to extract the VAT element.

3 VAT Registration and Deregistration

ActionRequirement
RegistrationEvery taxable person must register with FIRS within 6 months of commencing business, regardless of turnover threshold (Nigeria has no minimum VAT registration threshold for most businesses)
VAT CertificateFIRS issues a VAT registration certificate which must be displayed at the business premises
DeregistrationRequired when a business permanently ceases to make taxable supplies; must notify FIRS and file a final VAT return
PenaltiesFailure to register attracts penalties of ₦50,000 in the first month and ₦25,000 for each subsequent month of default

Withholding Tax (WHT)

An advance payment of income tax deducted at source — tested in both Nov 2025 and May 2025 diets.

1 What WHT Is and How It Works

Withholding tax is not a separate tax — it is an advance collection mechanism. The payer deducts tax at source from specified payments and remits it to the tax authority. The recipient then uses the WHT credit note issued by the tax authority to offset their final income tax liability.

Payment TypeTypical WHT Rate
Dividends10%
Interest10%
Rent10%
Royalties10%
Director's fees10%
Contracts/supplies (corporate)5%
Professional/consultancy fees5–10%

2 Rights and Obligations of Taxpayers Under WHT

  • Right to a WHT credit note from the tax authority, evidencing tax deducted
  • Right to use the credit note to offset final tax liability for the relevant year
  • Obligation to deduct and remit WHT at source if the payer is a designated withholding agent
  • Obligation to file WHT returns within the stipulated period (usually 21 days after the month of deduction)

3 Small Company Exemption from WHT Deduction

Under Section 105 of CITA, a small company (and equivalent unincorporated bodies) is exempt from the obligation to deduct WHT at source from transactions, where:

  • The company qualifies as a "small company" under the CIT classification (turnover ≤ ₦25 million)
  • The transaction is below a specified minimum threshold per supplier (de minimis rule)

4 Worked Example — Net WHT Payable/Receivable on Dividends and Interest

Submit Nigeria Limited (May/Nov style question)
WHT on Dividends: - WHT is deducted by the COMPANY when PAYING dividends to shareholders (10%) - WHT is also suffered by the COMPANY when RECEIVING dividends from another company (10%) — this is usually FINAL TAX, not creditable WHT on Interest: - WHT suffered on interest RECEIVED is creditable against final tax (10%) - WHT deducted when paying interest is remitted to FIRS Net position = WHT deducted/remitted on payments made - WHT credit notes held for tax suffered on income received If WHT remitted > WHT credits held → Net WHT PAYABLE If WHT credits held > WHT remitted → Net WHT RECEIVABLE (refund/offset)

Topic 07

Non-Interest (Islamic) Finance Taxation

A growing, newer topic in the ICAN syllabus reflecting Nigeria's Islamic finance sector — tested fully in Nov 2025 Q4.

1 The Non-Interest Finance (Taxation) Regulations 2022

Since Islamic finance does not use interest (riba is prohibited), Nigerian tax law treats the economic substance of each non-interest finance product, applying tax outcomes similar to conventional finance even though the legal form differs.

2 Tax Treatment of Key Islamic Finance Products

ProductStructureTax Treatment
MurabahaCost-plus sale — institution buys asset, resells to client at a mark-up, paid in instalmentsThe mark-up (profit margin) is treated like interest income/expense for tax purposes — taxable to the financier, deductible to the buyer over the instalment period. VAT applies on the underlying sale of goods.
Ijarah (lease)Institution buys asset, leases to client for rental; asset stays with the institutionTreated as an operating lease — rental income taxable to the lessor (institution); rental expense deductible to the lessee. Institution claims capital allowances since it retains ownership.
SukukAsset-backed certificates; "returns" derived from underlying lease/asset incomeReturns to Sukuk holders are treated analogously to interest for tax purposes (deductible to issuer, taxable to holder), per the Non-Interest Finance Regulations, to maintain parity with conventional bonds.
TakafulMutual/cooperative insurance; surplus distributed to participants based on contributionSurplus distributions to participants are treated similarly to dividends/distributions and may attract WHT; the takaful operator's underwriting surplus is assessed to tax like a conventional insurer.

3 Challenges in Applying Nigerian Tax Law to These Products

  • Conceptual mismatch: Nigerian tax law is built around interest-based concepts; non-interest products require interpretation by analogy, creating ambiguity
  • VAT complexity: Murabaha involves a sale of goods (the underlying asset) which can trigger VAT issues not present in conventional lending
  • Double taxation risk: Without careful regulation, the "double sale" structure in Murabaha could trigger VAT or stamp duty twice
  • Limited specialist tax personnel: Few FIRS officers or tax advisors are deeply trained in both Islamic finance structuring and Nigerian tax law
  • Evolving regulatory guidance: The Regulations are relatively new (2022) and case law/practice guidance is still developing

Topic 08

Tax Administration — FIRS Powers & Functions

Understanding the legal powers, enforcement tools, and functions of the Federal Inland Revenue Service — tested in Nov 2025 Q6.

1 Core Powers and Functions of FIRS

  • Assessment and collection of taxes specified under its enabling laws (CITA, PITA, VAT Act, etc.)
  • Account for all taxes collected to the Federation Account / relevant authorities
  • Issue assessments (including additional/back-duty assessments)
  • Conduct tax audits and investigations of taxpayers
  • Make regulations for the administration of tax laws under its purview
  • Adopt measures to identify, trace, and freeze assets connected with tax evasion
  • Collaborate with other government agencies on tax matters (e.g. Customs, CBN)
  • Issue Tax Identification Numbers (TIN) and maintain a taxpayer database

2 Enforcement Powers of FIRS

Key Enforcement Tools
- Power to access taxpayer's premises, books, and records - Power to demand information from third parties (banks, etc.) - Power to appoint agents (e.g. banks) to recover tax from a debtor's account — "substitution" - Power to distrain (seize) goods/property of a defaulting taxpayer - Power to prosecute tax offenders in court - Power to impose penalties and interest for late payment/filing

3 Power to Compound Offences

FIRS has the power to "compound" (settle out of court) certain tax offences. Instead of prosecuting a taxpayer through the courts, FIRS may accept a monetary settlement in lieu of prosecution, provided the taxpayer agrees to the terms. This power:

  • Saves time and resources for both FIRS and the courts
  • Is typically used for less serious offences
  • Requires the taxpayer's agreement — it is not unilaterally imposed
  • The settlement amount is usually a fraction of the potential fine/penalty under the law

Tax Legislation & Its Impact on the Nigerian Economy

A conceptual essay-style topic, frequently tested in Section C — tested directly in Nov 2025 Q5.

1 Importance of Tax Legislation to the Economy

  • Revenue generation: Primary source of government funding for infrastructure, security, education, healthcare
  • Economic regulation: Tax incentives can direct investment toward priority sectors (e.g. pioneer status, agriculture incentives)
  • Income redistribution: Progressive tax rates help reduce inequality
  • Fiscal policy tool: Tax rates can be adjusted to stimulate or cool down economic activity
  • Promotes compliance and formalisation of the economy through registration requirements

2 Challenges of Tax Legislation in Nigeria

  • Multiplicity of taxes: Overlapping federal, state, and local government taxes create compliance burden
  • Low tax-to-GDP ratio: Nigeria's tax revenue as a percentage of GDP remains low by international standards
  • Weak enforcement and large informal sector: Many businesses operate outside the tax net
  • Frequent legislative changes: Multiple Finance Acts in recent years create uncertainty for long-term planning
  • Tax evasion and avoidance: Sophisticated structuring by large taxpayers reduces the effective tax base
  • Administrative capacity constraints: Limited skilled personnel to audit and enforce compliance effectively

3 Impact of the Tax System on the Economy

  • Investment decisions: Tax holidays and incentives (e.g. pioneer status) attract foreign direct investment
  • Business cost structure: High effective tax rates can discourage formalisation and push businesses into the informal sector
  • Consumer behaviour: VAT and excise duties affect consumption patterns
  • Government's capacity to provide public goods: Tax revenue directly funds infrastructure and social services
  • International competitiveness: Tax rates relative to peer countries affect Nigeria's attractiveness for multinational operations

Tax Registration, Instalment Payment & Compliance

Practical compliance procedures every tax candidate must know — registration requirements, instalment payment conditions, and filing deadlines.

1 Registration Requirements for Sole Proprietors

To register for personal income tax as a sole proprietor in Nigeria:

  1. Register the business name with the Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC)
  2. Apply for a Tax Identification Number (TIN)
  3. Register with the relevant State tax authority as soon as business operations commence, by completing the prescribed form and forwarding it to the relevant tax authority
Key distinction: Sole proprietors pay PERSONAL income tax to STATE tax authorities (under PITA). Companies pay COMPANIES income tax to the FEDERAL authority (FIRS, under CITA). This is a foundational distinction tested repeatedly across diets.

2 Instalment Payment of Company Tax — Section 77(5)

Conditions for Instalment Payment (Finance Act 2019)
- An initial payment of at least 50% of the total tax liability must be made on or before the statutory due date for filing - The remaining balance must be settled within an agreed period (commonly within 2 months of the due date) - The taxpayer must have a satisfactory compliance history - Approval is at the discretion of the tax authority — not automatic

3 Filing Deadlines and Returns

Return TypeDeadline
Companies Income Tax (CIT) self-assessment6 months after the company's accounting year-end
Personal Income Tax (individual self-assessment)March 31 of the relevant year of assessment
VAT returns21 days after the end of the month the supply was made
Withholding tax returns21 days after the month of deduction
New companies — first CIT return18 months from incorporation or 6 months after the first accounting year end, whichever is earlier

4 Audit Process Overview (VAT Audit Example)

From the Nov 2025 Q3 scenario, a typical FIRS VAT audit follows this sequence:

  1. FIRS issues a notification letter with the list of required documents
  2. A preliminary meeting is scheduled with company management
  3. Field work is carried out — inspectors review accounting records on-site
  4. An exit interview is held to discuss preliminary findings (e.g. under-remittance identified)
  5. A final assessment (additional/back-duty assessment) is issued based on audit findings
  6. The taxpayer has a right to object and appeal the assessment within the statutory window if they disagree
