Topic 01

Introduction to Financial Management

What is FM, what does a financial manager do, and what objectives drive every decision?

1 What is Financial Management?

Financial Management is concerned with the planning, organising, directing and controlling of the financial activities of an organisation. It applies general management principles to the financial resources of a firm.

It covers three core decision areas:

  • Investment decisions – where to put the firm's money (capital budgeting)
  • Financing decisions – how to raise money (debt vs. equity)
  • Dividend decisions – how much profit to return to shareholders
Key Insight: Every FM decision affects the firm's value. The financial manager's job is to maximise that value within ethical and legal constraints.

2 Primary Objective: Shareholder Wealth Maximisation

The primary financial objective of a company is to maximise shareholder wealth, measured by the market price of shares.

This is preferred over "profit maximisation" because:

  • It considers the time value of money (future cash flows are discounted)
  • It accounts for risk (riskier projects need higher returns)
  • It is long-term focused (profit can be manipulated short-term)
⚠ Exam Watch: Questions often ask you to evaluate whether a decision will increase or decrease shareholder wealth. Always think: does this increase the share price / NPV?

3 Agency Problem

The agency problem arises when managers (agents) act in their own interest rather than the shareholders' (principals) interest. This creates agency costs — costs incurred to align interests.

Solutions include:

  • Performance-related pay and share options
  • Board of directors oversight
  • Auditing and disclosure requirements

Topic 02

Time Value of Money

The foundation of all valuation in FM. A naira today is worth more than a naira tomorrow.

1 Why Money Has Time Value

A sum of money received today is worth more than the same amount received in the future because:

  • It can be invested to earn a return
  • Inflation erodes purchasing power over time
  • There is uncertainty about receiving future cash

2 Future Value (Compounding)

If you invest a sum today, how much will it be worth in the future?

Future Value Formula
FV = PV × (1 + r)ⁿ Where: PV = Present Value (amount invested today) r = Interest rate per period n = Number of periods

Example: You invest ₦500,000 today at 10% per annum for 3 years.

FV = 500,000 × (1.10)³ = 500,000 × 1.331 = ₦665,500

3 Present Value (Discounting)

Discounting is the reverse of compounding. It tells us what a future cash flow is worth in today's terms.

Present Value Formula
PV = FV ÷ (1 + r)ⁿ Or using the Discount Factor: PV = FV × [1 ÷ (1 + r)ⁿ] The term [1 ÷ (1 + r)ⁿ] is the DISCOUNT FACTOR
Key: In ICAN exams you will be given discount factor tables. Always use them. Check whether the question asks for PV of a lump sum or PV of an annuity.

4 Annuities and Perpetuities

An annuity is a series of equal cash flows at regular intervals.

Annuity Formula (PV of Annuity)
PV = A × [(1 – (1+r)⁻ⁿ) ÷ r] Where A = the annual cash flow amount Perpetuity (annuity lasting forever): PV = A ÷ r Growing Perpetuity: PV = A ÷ (r – g) where g = growth rate
⚠ Exam Watch: The Gordon Growth Model for share valuation uses the growing perpetuity formula. Know it cold.

5 Nominal vs. Real Interest Rates

Fisher Effect
(1 + nominal rate) = (1 + real rate) × (1 + inflation rate) Simplified (approximation): Nominal rate ≈ Real rate + Inflation rate

Use real rates to discount real (inflation-adjusted) cash flows, and nominal rates to discount nominal cash flows. Mixing them is one of the most common exam errors.

Topic 03

Capital Budgeting & Investment Appraisal

How should a firm decide which long-term investments to accept? This is one of the highest-weighted areas in ICAN FM.

1 Net Present Value (NPV)

NPV is the gold standard appraisal technique. It calculates the total value added to a firm by a project.

NPV Formula
NPV = Σ [Cₜ ÷ (1 + r)ᵗ] – Initial Investment Where: Cₜ = Cash inflow in year t r = Cost of capital (discount rate) t = Year Decision Rule: NPV > 0 → ACCEPT (adds value) NPV < 0 → REJECT (destroys value) NPV = 0 → Indifferent
Why NPV is the best method: It considers the time value of money, uses cash flows (not profit), accounts for risk via the discount rate, and gives an absolute measure of wealth added.

2 Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

IRR is the discount rate at which NPV = 0. It is the project's own rate of return.

IRR Interpolation Formula (used when no formula solver)
IRR = L + [NPV_L ÷ (NPV_L – NPV_H)] × (H – L) Where: L = Lower discount rate (positive NPV) H = Higher discount rate (negative NPV) NPV_L = NPV at lower rate NPV_H = NPV at higher rate Decision Rule: IRR > Cost of Capital → ACCEPT IRR < Cost of Capital → REJECT
⚠ Limitation of IRR: IRR can give multiple answers with non-conventional cash flows. It also assumes reinvestment at IRR rate (unrealistic). NPV is always preferred where they conflict.

3 Payback Period

Payback is the time taken to recover the initial investment from cash inflows.

  • Simple to understand and widely used in practice
  • Ignores time value of money and cash flows after payback
  • Discounted Payback corrects for time value but still ignores post-payback flows
Decision: Accept if payback period is within the firm's target. Shorter payback = less risk in unstable environments like Nigeria.

4 Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

ARR Formula
ARR = (Average Annual Accounting Profit ÷ Average Investment) × 100 Average Investment = (Initial Cost + Scrap Value) ÷ 2 Limitation: Uses profit (not cash), ignores time value of money

5 Relevant Cash Flows in Appraisal

Only incremental, future cash flows are relevant. Exclude:

  • Sunk costs — already spent, cannot be recovered
  • Allocated overheads — not incremental unless additional

Include:

  • Opportunity costs (e.g. lost rental income)
  • Tax effects and capital allowances
  • Working capital changes
  • Residual/scrap value at project end

Topic 04

Cost of Capital & WACC

The discount rate used in NPV is the firm's cost of capital. Getting this right is critical to every valuation.

1 Cost of Equity (Ke)

The return required by ordinary shareholders. Two main models:

Dividend Growth Model (Gordon Growth)
Ke = (D₁ ÷ P₀) + g Where: D₁ = Next year's expected dividend = D₀ × (1 + g) P₀ = Current market price (ex-dividend) g = Constant annual dividend growth rate
Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)
Ke = Rf + β(Rm – Rf) Where: Rf = Risk-free rate β = Beta (systematic risk of the company) Rm = Expected market return (Rm – Rf) = Equity risk premium (ERP)
CAPM vs DGM: CAPM is theoretically superior but needs beta. DGM is simpler but assumes constant growth. ICAN exams test both — recognise which inputs you have.

2 Cost of Debt (Kd)

Debt is cheaper than equity because:

  • Interest is tax deductible (tax shield)
  • Debt holders have priority over equity in liquidation
After-tax Cost of Irredeemable Debt
Kd = [Interest × (1 – Tax Rate)] ÷ Market Value of Debt For Redeemable Debt, use IRR of the debt cash flows: Cash flows = Interest (after tax) + Redemption Value Discount to find Kd (the IRR)

3 Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)

WACC is the overall cost of finance, weighted by the proportion of each source.

WACC Formula
WACC = [Ke × Ve/(Ve+Vd)] + [Kd × Vd/(Ve+Vd)] Where: Ve = Market value of equity Vd = Market value of debt Ke = Cost of equity Kd = After-tax cost of debt Use MARKET VALUES (not book values) for weighting.
⚠ Critical Exam Point: WACC is only used as the discount rate when a new project has the SAME risk and SAME capital structure as the existing firm. For different risk profiles, use CAPM with a project-specific beta.

Topic 05

Capital Structure

How much debt vs. equity should a firm use? Does it actually matter?

1 The Capital Structure Debate

Capital structure refers to the mix of debt and equity a firm uses to finance its assets. The central question: Is there an optimal debt-to-equity ratio that maximises firm value?

TheoryKey Claim
Modigliani & Miller (No Tax)Capital structure is irrelevant — firm value is unchanged
M&M with TaxDebt is beneficial due to tax shield; 100% debt is theoretically optimal
Trade-off TheoryOptimal structure balances tax shield benefits vs. bankruptcy costs
Pecking Order TheoryFirms prefer retained earnings → debt → equity (in that order)

2 Modigliani & Miller (M&M) — Key Propositions

M&M Proposition I (with tax)
Value of Levered Firm = Value of Unlevered Firm + PV of Tax Shield PV of Tax Shield = Debt × Tax Rate (assuming permanent debt)
M&M Proposition II (with tax)
Ke (levered) = Ke (unlevered) + [Ke(unlevered) – Kd] × (Vd/Ve) × (1 – T) As a firm takes on more debt, the cost of equity rises (financial risk increases for shareholders)
The M&M world assumes: No transaction costs, no bankruptcy risk, equal access to information. Real-world capital structure decisions must account for all these factors.

3 Financial Gearing

Gearing Ratios
Capital Gearing = Debt ÷ (Debt + Equity) × 100% OR Debt-to-Equity = Long-term Debt ÷ Equity × 100% Income Gearing (Interest Cover) = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense

High gearing = higher financial risk but potential for higher returns to equity holders (financial leverage effect).

Topic 06

Working Capital Management

Managing the short-term financial health of a business — cash, receivables, inventory, and payables.

1 The Working Capital Cycle

The Cash Operating Cycle (COC) measures how long it takes to convert cash invested in operations back into cash.

Cash Operating Cycle
COC = Inventory Days + Receivables Days – Payables Days Inventory Days = (Inventory ÷ Cost of Sales) × 365 Receivables Days = (Trade Receivables ÷ Revenue) × 365 Payables Days = (Trade Payables ÷ Cost of Sales) × 365 Shorter COC = less cash tied up = better liquidity

2 Inventory Management

Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)
EOQ = √[(2 × D × Co) ÷ Ch] Where: D = Annual demand (units) Co = Cost per order Ch = Holding cost per unit per year Reorder Level = Lead time demand + Safety stock

3 Receivables Management

Key decisions: Should we offer early settlement discounts? Should we factor our receivables?

Evaluating an Early Settlement Discount
Annual Cost of Discount = [d% ÷ (100 – d%)] × [365 ÷ (N – D)] Where: d = discount percentage N = normal credit period (days) D = discount period (days) Compare to cost of borrowing / overdraft rate. If cost of discount > cost of borrowing → do NOT offer discount

4 Cash Management Models

ModelBest ForKey Feature
Baumol ModelPredictable, steady cash flowsLike EOQ but for cash
Miller-Orr ModelUncertain / variable cash flowsSets upper & lower limits; return point

Topic 07

Dividend Policy

Does paying dividends increase, decrease, or have no effect on firm value?

1 Dividend Theories

TheoryViewProponents
Irrelevance TheoryDividend policy does NOT affect firm valueModigliani & Miller
Bird-in-Hand TheoryInvestors prefer dividends now (certainty); high dividends = higher valueGordon & Lintner
Tax Preference TheoryInvestors prefer capital gains (taxed lower) over dividendsLitzenberger & Ramaswamy
Signalling TheoryDividend changes signal management's confidence in future earningsRoss
Clientele EffectDifferent investors prefer different policies; changing policy changes investor baseM&M

2 Types of Dividend Policies

  • Stable / Constant Dividend: Same payout each year — signals stability
  • Constant Payout Ratio: Fixed % of earnings — fluctuates with profit
  • Residual Policy: Pay dividends only after all positive NPV projects are funded
  • Zero Dividend: Retain all earnings for reinvestment

3 Share Repurchase vs. Cash Dividend

In an M&M world, share buyback and cash dividend are equivalent in value. In practice:

  • Buybacks offer more flexibility — not expected to repeat each year
  • Buybacks can signal that shares are undervalued
  • Cash dividends are perceived as more committed/reliable by some investors

Topic 08

Risk & Return / Portfolio Theory

Higher risk demands higher return. But not all risk is rewarded — only systematic risk matters in a well-diversified portfolio.

1 Types of Risk

TypeDefinitionCan be Diversified?
Systematic (Market) RiskAffects all firms (e.g. interest rates, inflation, recession)No
Unsystematic (Specific) RiskAffects one firm/industry (e.g. strike action, product recall)Yes — by diversifying
Key Principle: Rational investors diversify away unsystematic risk. The market only rewards systematic risk. Beta (β) measures systematic risk.

2 Beta (β) Interpretation

Beta Interpretation
β = 1.0 → moves exactly with the market β > 1.0 → more volatile than market (higher risk, higher return) β < 1.0 → less volatile than market (defensive stock) β = 0 → risk-free asset (e.g. government treasury bill)

3 Adjusting Beta for Capital Structure (Asset Beta)

When a firm changes its gearing, its equity beta changes. We use asset beta (ungeared beta) as the underlying business risk.

Ungearing / Regearing Beta (M&M Formula)
βa = βe × [Ve ÷ (Ve + Vd(1–T))] To regear (find new equity beta): βe = βa × [(Ve + Vd(1–T)) ÷ Ve] Where: βa = Asset beta (ungeared) βe = Equity beta (geared) T = Corporation tax rate
⚠ Exam Trap: When appraising a project in a different business area, find the proxy company's asset beta, then regear to your firm's capital structure before using CAPM.

Where does a firm get its money from, and what are the characteristics, costs, and risks of each source?

2 Rights Issues

A rights issue offers existing shareholders the right to buy new shares at a discount to market price, in proportion to existing holdings.

Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP)
TERP = [(N × P) + Issue Price] ÷ (N + 1) Where: N = Number of existing shares per right P = Current market price Value of Right = TERP – Issue Price of new shares
Key Point: Rights issues do not make shareholders better or worse off in theory — the discount is offset by more shares. However, in practice they can signal positive investment opportunities.

  • Musharaka — Joint venture / partnership financing
  • Mudaraba — Profit-sharing arrangement; bank funds, entrepreneur manages

Business & Share Valuation

How do you value a company for a merger, acquisition, or listing? Different methods suit different situations.

1 Asset-Based Valuation

Values the firm based on the net assets on its balance sheet.

MethodUsesLimitation
Book ValueHistorical cost of assets less liabilitiesIgnores intangibles and market values
Net Realisable ValueWhat assets would fetch if soldAssumes liquidation — not going concern
Replacement CostWhat it costs to replace assets todayCostly and complex to calculate

2 Income-Based Valuation

P/E Ratio Method
Share Value = EPS × P/E Ratio Use the P/E of a comparable listed company. EPS = Profit After Tax ÷ Number of Shares
Earnings Yield Method
Share Value = EPS ÷ Earnings Yield Earnings Yield = 1 ÷ P/E Ratio

3 Dividend Valuation Model (DVM)

Gordon Growth Model (Constant Growth)
P₀ = D₁ ÷ (Ke – g) = D₀(1 + g) ÷ (Ke – g) Only valid when: g < Ke For a company with no growth: P₀ = D ÷ Ke

4 Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Valuation

The most theoretically sound method. Value = PV of all future free cash flows.

Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF)
FCFF = Operating Profit after tax + Depreciation/Amortisation – Capital Expenditure – Increase in Working Capital Discount at WACC to get Enterprise Value Enterprise Value – Net Debt = Equity Value Equity Value ÷ No. of Shares = Share Price
