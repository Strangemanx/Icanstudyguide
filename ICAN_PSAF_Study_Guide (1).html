Topic 01

IPSAS — International Public Sector Accounting Standards

IPSAS provides the conceptual and technical framework for government financial reporting. Understanding the differences from IFRS is essential.

1 What is IPSAS?

IPSAS are accounting standards developed by the International Public Sector Accounting Standards Board (IPSASB) — a body of the International Federation of Accountants (IFAC). They are designed for use by national, state, and local governments and other public sector entities around the world.

Nigeria adopted IPSAS through the Financial Reporting Council (FRC) Act 2011, which mandated all public sector entities at federal and state levels to prepare financial statements in accordance with IPSAS.

2 Two Bases of IPSAS

BasisWhen Revenue/Expenditure RecognisedUsed For
Cash Basis IPSASWhen cash is received or paid (no accruals)Transitional governments; less developed public sector environments
Accrual Basis IPSASWhen transactions occur, regardless of cash flowFully developed public financial management systems; Nigeria's target
Nigeria's trajectory: Federal Government migrated to accrual-basis IPSAS from 2016. State governments are at varying stages. The accrual basis gives a more complete picture of a government's assets, liabilities and obligations.

3 Key Differences: IPSAS vs IFRS

FeatureIPSAS (Public Sector)IFRS (Private Sector)
Primary objectiveAccountability and stewardship of public resourcesDecision-useful information for investors
Revenue recognitionNon-exchange revenue (taxes, grants, fines) treated differently — IPSAS 23IFRS 15 based on contract/performance obligations
MeasurementHistorical cost common; fair value allowedExtensive fair value measurement
Reporting entityWhole-of-government consolidated statementsLegal entity or group
Budget reportingBudget vs actual comparison required (IPSAS 24)No requirement to present budgets

4 Non-Exchange Revenue (IPSAS 23)

Non-exchange transactions are those where a government receives value without directly giving approximately equal value in return (e.g. taxes, grants, fines, donations).

IPSAS 23 — Non-Exchange Revenue Recognition
An asset (cash, receivable) is recognised when: (i) it is probable that future economic benefits will flow to the entity (ii) the fair value can be reliably measured Revenue is recognised when there is no condition attached → recognise immediately on receipt/right to receive If a condition IS attached (e.g. must spend on a specified purpose): → recognise as a liability first → transfer to revenue only as the condition is satisfied

Topic 02

Government Budgeting — Types, Process & Control

The budget is the government's most powerful planning and control tool. Different budgeting techniques have specific strengths and weaknesses — all examinable.

1 Types of Government Budgets

TypeKey FeatureAdvantageLimitation
IncrementalPrevious year's budget + % increaseSimple, quickPreserves inefficiencies; never questions base spending
Zero-Based (ZBB)Every item justified from scratch each yearEliminates waste; forces priority reviewTime-consuming; expensive to implement
Programme/PerformanceOrganised by government programmes and outcomesLinks spending to results; improves accountabilityDifficult to set measurable output targets
Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF)3-year rolling budget aligned to policyImproved fiscal discipline; long-term planningForecasting uncertainty; political pressure

2 The Budget Cycle in Nigeria

  1. Budget Call Circular: Ministry of Finance/Budget Office issues guidelines and ceilings to MDAs
  2. MDA Submissions: Ministries, Departments, and Agencies prepare and submit their estimates
  3. Budget Office Coordination: Consolidates submissions; aligns with macro-fiscal framework
  4. Executive Budget: President/Governor presents the Appropriation Bill to the Legislature
  5. Legislative Approval: National Assembly/State House reviews, amends, and passes the Appropriation Act
  6. Implementation: Warrant releases by Ministry of Finance; expenditure by MDAs
  7. Monitoring & Evaluation: Budget Office tracks performance; audit by Auditor-General

3 Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF)

The MTEF is a three-year rolling fiscal framework required under the Fiscal Responsibility Act 2007. It consists of:

  • Macro-Fiscal Framework: GDP growth, inflation, oil price benchmark, revenue projections
  • Medium-Term Sector Strategies (MTSS): Sector-level spending plans aligned to national priorities
  • Medium-Term Budget Framework (MTBF): Aggregate spending ceilings by MDA over 3 years
Nigeria's MTEF requirement: Section 11 of the Fiscal Responsibility Act 2007 mandates the FGN to prepare a 3-year MTEF and forward it to the National Assembly not later than the second Monday in April each year.

Topic 04

Consolidated Revenue Fund & Other Public Funds

The Consolidated Revenue Fund is the central government account under the Constitution. Understanding its structure and the funds around it is core PSAF knowledge.

1 Consolidated Revenue Fund (CRF)

The CRF is the main government account established by Section 80 of the 1999 Constitution. All revenues and other money raised or received by the Federal Government shall be paid into the CRF.

Money can only be withdrawn from the CRF in three ways:

  1. To meet expenditure charged on the CRF (statutory expenditure)
  2. Where authorised by an Appropriation Act or Supplementary Appropriation Act
  3. Under a vote on account (not exceeding 1/2 of the previous year's budget) — to fund government before the Appropriation Act is passed
Constitutional basis: CRF = Section 80. Federation Account = Section 162. Both are separate accounts serving different purposes.

2 Other Key Government Funds

FundPurposeLegal Basis
Federation AccountPool into which all federal collectible revenues are paid before sharing among the 3 tiersSection 162, 1999 Constitution
Contingency FundCaters for urgent/unforeseen expenditure when no provision exists in the Appropriation ActSection 83, 1999 Constitution
Stabilisation FundSavings during high-revenue periods to cushion against revenue shortfallsRMAFC Act
Ecological FundAddresses environmental challenges (erosion, flooding, desertification)RMAFC sharing formula
Development Fund / Capital FundFinances capital projects in the development budgetAnnual Appropriation Act

3 Warrant System — Releasing Funds

Even after the Appropriation Act is passed, funds are not automatically available to MDAs. They are released through a warrant system:

  • General Warrant: Authority to spend recurrent votes — issued by Accountant-General
  • Specific Warrant: For capital projects or when a general warrant is insufficient
  • Imprest Warrant: Small, petty cash-type advance for minor/immediate expenditures
  • Virement: Transfer of appropriated funds between sub-heads within the same head, subject to approval

Topic 05

Public Accounts — Officers, Books & Responsibilities

Who are the key accounting officers in Nigeria's public sector? What records must they maintain? Tested regularly in PSAF exams.

1 Key Accounting Officers

OfficerRole
Accountant-General of the Federation (AGF)Chief accounting officer of the Federation; maintains the government's general ledger; issues warrants; produces consolidated annual accounts
Auditor-General for the Federation (AuGF)Independent auditor of all public accounts; reports to the National Assembly; cannot be removed by the Executive
Accounting Officer (Permanent Secretary/HoA)Responsible for the accounts of their MDA; signs off on expenditure; answers to PAC for any irregularities
Sub-Accounting OfficerHeads lower units; responsible to Accounting Officer for financial transactions under them
Revenue CollectorReceives government revenue; issues official receipts; remits to designated accounts

2 Principal Books of Account

  • Cash Book: Primary book recording all cash receipts and payments
  • Vote Book / Expenditure Ledger: Tracks appropriation, warrants issued, and expenditure per vote head
  • Revenue Register: Records all amounts due and collected for each revenue head
  • Store/Inventory Records: Documents government assets, stores received and issued
  • Register of Fixed Assets: Inventories non-current assets of the government
  • Advance/Loan Registers: Tracks advances made to staff or third parties and repayments
Vote Book discipline: No expenditure should exceed the amount authorised by warrant. The Vote Book is the key control preventing over-expenditure of appropriated funds.

3 The Public Accounts Committee (PAC)

The PAC is a committee of the legislature (National Assembly or State House of Assembly) that examines the Auditor-General's report and calls Accounting Officers to account for any financial irregularities, waste, or misapplication of public funds.

  • The PAC can summon any Accounting Officer to appear and explain irregularities
  • It has power to recommend surcharges, prosecution, or disciplinary action
  • It is the primary legislative oversight mechanism for public financial accountability

Government Financial Statements under IPSAS

The complete set of financial statements a government entity must prepare — and what each one shows.

1 Components of Government Financial Statements (Accrual IPSAS)

StatementIPSAS Private Sector EquivalentWhat It Shows
Statement of Financial PositionBalance Sheet (IAS 1)Assets, liabilities and net assets/equity of the government entity
Statement of Financial PerformanceIncome Statement / P&LRevenue and expenses for the period; surplus or deficit
Statement of Changes in Net Assets/EquityStatement of Changes in EquityMovements in government's net assets during the year
Cash Flow StatementStatement of Cash Flows (IAS 7)Cash inflows and outflows; classified as operating, investing, financing
Statement of Comparison of Budget and Actual AmountsNo equivalent in IFRSCompares original/final approved budget to actual outturn (IPSAS 24)
Notes to the Financial StatementsNotesAccounting policies and detailed disclosures

2 Statement of Financial Performance — Key Lines

Government Statement of Financial Performance
REVENUE: Tax revenue (non-exchange) X Non-tax revenue (fees, fines, licences) X Transfers/grants from other government entities X Aid and donations X TOTAL REVENUE X EXPENSES: Employee costs (salaries, pensions) (X) Goods and services (X) Depreciation and amortisation (X) Finance costs (interest on public debt) (X) Transfer payments (social benefits, subsidies) (X) TOTAL EXPENSES (X) SURPLUS / (DEFICIT) FOR THE PERIOD X
Surplus vs Profit: Government entities do not have "profit" — they have a surplus (revenues exceed expenses) or deficit (expenses exceed revenues). The deficit is financed by borrowing or drawing down reserves.

3 Budget vs Actual Comparison (IPSAS 24)

IPSAS 24 requires entities that make their budgets publicly available to present a comparison of budget and actual amounts. The comparison must show:

  • Original budget — the initial approved budget
  • Final budget — the original budget adjusted for virements, supplementary estimates
  • Actual amounts — on the same basis as the budget (often cash basis)
  • Variance — final budget vs actual, with explanations for material differences

Public Procurement — PPA 2007

The Public Procurement Act 2007 governs how government contracts are awarded. Tested regularly — especially thresholds, methods, and the role of BPP.

1 The Bureau of Public Procurement (BPP)

The BPP is established under the Public Procurement Act (PPA) 2007 as the regulatory authority for public procurement. Its key functions include:

  • Formulating general policies on public procurement
  • Setting procurement standards and procedures
  • Publishing standard bidding documents
  • Publishing the Monthly Price Intelligence Report to guide procurement costs
  • Certifying "Certificate of No Objection" for contracts above the threshold requiring BPP review
  • Training procurement officers and building institutional capacity
  • Maintaining a database of federal contractors and suppliers (vendor register)

2 Procurement Methods under PPA 2007

MethodWhen UsedKey Feature
Open Competitive Bidding (OCB)Default method for most contracts above thresholdPublic advertisement; widest competition; most transparent
Restricted TenderingLimited suppliers exist; specialised goods/servicesInvitation to a pre-selected list of qualified bidders
Request for Quotations (RFQ)Small value contracts below thresholdsMinimum 3 quotations; no public advertisement needed
Direct Procurement (Single Source)Emergency; only one supplier; proprietary goodsBypasses competition; requires BPP approval; strictly regulated
Expression of Interest (EOI)Pre-qualification stage for large/complex contractsFilters bidders before inviting full bids
⚠ Exam focus: Direct procurement (single-source) is the most abused method in practice. The conditions for its valid use — emergency, sole supplier, national security, extension of existing contract — are frequently tested.

3 Procurement Thresholds (Approving Authorities)

Federal Procurement Approval Thresholds (approximate)
Goods: MDA Procurement Committee: Up to ₦2.5 million Ministerial Tender Board: ₦2.5m – ₦100m Federal Executive Council (FEC):Above ₦100 million Works/Construction: MDA: Up to ₦10 million Ministerial Tender Board: ₦10m – ₦500m FEC: Above ₦500 million Note: All contracts above ₦100m also require BPP "No Objection" certificate before award.

Topic 08

Public Sector Internal Control & Audit

Effective internal control and audit are the backbone of accountability in the public sector — tested in virtually every PSAF exam diet.

1 Internal Control in the Public Sector

Internal controls in government entities exist to:

  • Ensure compliance with laws, regulations, and government financial regulations (GFRs)
  • Safeguard public assets from theft, waste, and misuse
  • Promote efficiency and economy in government operations
  • Ensure accuracy and reliability of financial information
  • Prevent and detect fraud and corruption
Control TypeExample in Public Sector
Authorisation controlsExpenditure requires warrant before payment; procurement requires approvals per PPA thresholds
Segregation of dutiesThe person who raises a payment voucher must be different from the person who approves and signs the cheque
Physical controlsSecured treasury, lockable stores, register of fixed assets
Reconciliation controlsMonthly bank reconciliation by Accountant; Vote Book reconciliation
DocumentationPayment vouchers, receipts, stores records, delivery notes

2 The Office of the Auditor-General (OAuGF)

The Auditor-General for the Federation (AuGF) is the external auditor of all federal public accounts. Key features:

  • Established under Section 85–86, 1999 Constitution
  • Independent of the Executive — appointed by the President on recommendation of the Federal Civil Service Commission, confirmed by the Senate
  • Cannot be removed by the President alone — requires 2/3 of the Senate
  • Has access to all government books, accounts, and records
  • Reports to the National Assembly within 90 days of receiving the AGF's accounts
  • Cannot express opinion on the economy, policy, or wisdom of government expenditure — only on its legality and regularity

3 Value for Money (VFM) Audit

Beyond financial regularity, modern public sector audit also examines Value for Money — the 3Es:

  • Economy: Are resources acquired at minimum cost for the required quality?
  • Efficiency: Are resources converted into outputs with minimum waste?
  • Effectiveness: Do the outputs actually achieve the intended policy outcomes?
VFM vs Financial Audit: Financial audit checks that money was spent lawfully and accurately recorded. VFM audit checks whether the spending actually delivered what it was supposed to — a higher-level accountability assessment.

Topic 09

Fiscal Responsibility Act 2007

Nigeria's Fiscal Responsibility Act (FRA) sets the rules for prudent management of the country's finances — a compulsory knowledge area for PSAF.

1 Key Objectives of the FRA 2007

  • Promote transparency and accountability in fiscal operations
  • Ensure prudent management of national resources
  • Prevent fiscal irresponsibility and excessive deficits
  • Align annual budgets with a medium-term fiscal framework
  • Protect future generations from unsustainable debt burden

2 Key Provisions of the FRA 2007

SectionProvision
Sec. 11FGN must prepare a 3-year MTEF and forward to NASS by 2nd Monday in April
Sec. 12Annual budget must be consistent with the approved MTEF
Sec. 16Total expenditure must not exceed aggregate revenue plus a deficit not exceeding 3% of GDP
Sec. 21FGN must maintain a Fiscal Savings Fund (Excess Crude Account) when oil revenues exceed benchmark
Sec. 27All government borrowings must be used for capital expenditure, not recurrent spending
Sec. 44States and LGAs must also comply with fiscal responsibility provisions

3 The Excess Crude Account (ECA) / Sovereign Wealth Fund

When oil revenue exceeds the benchmark price built into the budget, the surplus goes into the Excess Crude Account (ECA) — Nigeria's fiscal savings buffer. This helps stabilise the budget when oil prices fall below the benchmark.

The Nigeria Sovereign Investment Authority (NSIA) was established to manage the Nigerian Sovereign Wealth Fund, which consists of three tranches:

  • Stabilisation Fund: Short-term buffer to cushion revenue shocks
  • Future Generations Fund: Long-term savings invested for future Nigerians
  • Nigeria Infrastructure Fund: Domestic infrastructure investments

Public Debt Management

How government borrowing is authorised, managed, and monitored — including the role of the DMO and Nigeria's debt sustainability framework.

1 The Debt Management Office (DMO)

The DMO was established by the Debt Management Office (Establishment) Act 2003. Its key functions:

  • Maintain a comprehensive database of all government debt (domestic and external)
  • Prepare and implement the government's Medium-Term Debt Management Strategy (MTDS)
  • Advise the government on the terms and conditions of new borrowing
  • Issue and manage FGN Bonds, Treasury Bills, and Sukuk on behalf of the FGN
  • Negotiate and manage external loans (multilateral, bilateral, commercial)
  • Monitor and report on Nigeria's debt portfolio and debt sustainability

2 Types of Government Debt

TypeSourceExample
Domestic DebtBorrowed from within NigeriaFGN Bonds, Treasury Bills, Promissory Notes
External Debt (Multilateral)International development institutionsWorld Bank, AfDB, IMF loans
External Debt (Bilateral)Government-to-government loansChina Exim Bank, Afreximbank
External Debt (Commercial)International capital marketsEurobonds, Diaspora Bonds
Contingent LiabilitiesNot yet debt, but potential obligationsGovernment guarantees on state borrowings

3 Debt Sustainability Analysis (DSA)

Debt sustainability refers to a government's ability to meet its current and future debt obligations without requiring exceptional financing or defaulting. Key ratios used:

Key Debt Sustainability Indicators
Debt-to-GDP Ratio = Total Public Debt / Nominal GDP × 100% (Nigeria's IMF benchmark ceiling: typically 40–55% for emerging economies) Debt Service-to-Revenue = Total Debt Service / Total Revenue × 100% (Nigeria's concern: this ratio has been very high — over 80% in recent years, meaning most revenue goes to debt repayment) External Debt-to-Exports = External Debt / Total Export Earnings × 100% (Measures ability to earn foreign exchange to service external debt)
Nigeria's challenge: While Nigeria's debt-to-GDP ratio appears moderate by international standards, its debt service-to-revenue ratio is extremely high, meaning a disproportionate share of government revenue goes to paying debt rather than delivering services. This is the key fiscal risk for Nigeria.

4 Constitutional Provisions on Borrowing

  • Section 21 FRA: All borrowings must be for capital expenditure only
  • Section 41 FRA: External borrowing must be approved by the National Assembly
  • The Annual Borrowing Plan (part of the MTEF/budget) must be laid before and approved by NASS
  • State governments may borrow but the FGN cannot guarantee state debt without National Assembly approval
