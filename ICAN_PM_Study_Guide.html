Topic 01

Introduction to Performance Management

What PM is about, how costs behave, and the key decision-making framework that runs through every topic.

1 What is Performance Management?

Performance Management is about providing information to managers to help them plan, control, and make decisions within an organisation. It sits at the intersection of management accounting and strategy.

The key questions PM answers:

  • Planning: What should we produce? At what cost?
  • Control: Are we achieving our targets? Where are variances coming from?
  • Decision-making: Should we accept this order? Which product is most profitable?

2 Cost Classification — The Foundation

Cost TypeBehaviourExample
Variable CostChanges proportionally with activityDirect materials, direct labour
Fixed CostUnchanged within a relevant rangeRent, insurance
Semi-Variable (Mixed)Fixed element + variable elementPower, telephone
Step FixedFixed, but jumps at certain activity levelsSupervisors — one extra hired per 100k units
🎯 May 2025 — Q6 (Flexible Budgets)

The flexible budget question used a step cost for supervision — fixed up to 100,000 units, then an assistant supervisor added at N1,280,000 for higher levels. Always check if a cost is truly fixed or is a step function.

3 High-Low Method (Separating Fixed and Variable)

High-Low Method
Variable cost per unit = (Highest cost – Lowest cost) / (Highest activity – Lowest activity) Fixed cost = Total cost at either level – (Variable cost per unit × Activity level) Example (from May 2025 Q6): Power at 160,000 units = ₦1,920,000 Power at 80,000 units = ₦1,440,000 Variable rate = (1,920,000 – 1,440,000) / (160,000 – 80,000) = 480,000 / 80,000 = ₦6 per unit Fixed = 1,440,000 – (6 × 80,000) = ₦960,000

Topic 02

Relevant Costing

The most important concept in PM decision-making. This was the compulsory Q1 in May 2025 — you must master it.

1 What Makes a Cost Relevant?

A relevant cost is one that will be affected by a decision. To be relevant, a cost must be:

  • Future — not already spent
  • Incremental — arises as a direct result of the decision
  • Cash flow — not an accounting allocation
Irrelevant costs (always exclude): Sunk costs (already spent), absorbed/allocated overheads (not incremental), depreciation (non-cash), committed costs (will be paid regardless).

2 Relevant Cost of Materials

SituationRelevant Cost
Must be bought in specificallyCurrent purchase price
In inventory, will be replacedCurrent replacement cost
In inventory, has alternative useHigher of: net realisable value OR opportunity cost of alternative use
In inventory, no other useNet realisable value (scrap/resale price)
🎯 May 2025 Q1 — Material B

5,000 sq metres in inventory. No other use. Could be sold for ₦20/sqm. Current purchase price is ₦50/sqm. Relevant cost = ₦20 × 5,000 = ₦100,000 (opportunity cost = selling price foregone). Original cost of ₦40/sqm (1,000,000/25,000) is a sunk cost — irrelevant.

3 Relevant Cost of Labour

SituationRelevant Cost
Spare/idle capacityZero (labour is paid regardless)
Must be recruited externallyExternal recruitment rate
Transferred from another job requiring replacementReplacement cost of substitute
Overtime requiredOvertime premium only (if basic pay already committed)
🎯 May 2025 Q1 — Skilled Labour

External rate = ₦120/hr. Internal rate = ₦100/hr. Replacement cost for existing project = ₦110/hr. Relevant cost = ₦110/hr (the incremental cost of using existing workers is replacing them on their current project — the existing ₦100 salary is committed regardless).

4 Relevant Cost of Equipment / Machinery

When a machine can be hired or purchased, compare the net relevant cost of each option:

May 2025 Q1 — Machine Decision
Hire cost: ₦40,000 × 10 weeks = ₦400,000 Buy option net cost: Purchase price: ₦2,500,000 Less: Residual value: (₦1,500,000) Net cost: ₦1,000,000 Less: Rental income from hiring out: ₦25,000 × 30 weeks = (₦750,000) Net relevant cost: ₦250,000 ← CHEAPER → BUY Running cost of machine = ₦7,200 × 10 weeks = ₦72,000 (incurred by the user — always relevant) Depreciation = NOT relevant (non-cash, accounting entry)

5 Why Financial Reports Will Differ from Relevant Cost Profit

This was asked directly in May 2025 Q1(b). Key reasons:

  • Financial accounts will charge depreciation (relevant costing ignores it — non-cash)
  • Financial accounts will absorb general overheads (relevant costing excludes non-incremental ones)
  • Financial accounts use FIFO/weighted average for materials, not opportunity cost
  • Labour is charged at standard rates, not at opportunity cost

Topic 03

Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) Analysis

Understand the relationship between cost, volume and profit — and how contribution drives every short-term decision.

1 Contribution — The Central Concept

Contribution
Contribution = Sales Revenue – Variable Costs Contribution per unit = Selling Price – Variable Cost per unit Contribution to Sales Ratio (C/S ratio) = Contribution / Revenue × 100% Example (May 2025 Q2 — Adebat Cleaning): Complete wash: price ₦1,000, C/S ratio = 45% → contribution = ₦450/car Mini wash: price ₦500, C/S ratio = 56% → contribution = ₦280/car
Why contribution matters: Fixed costs are irrelevant for most short-term decisions. Contribution is what's left to cover fixed costs and generate profit. Higher contribution per unit of scarce resource = better product to prioritise.

2 Break-Even Analysis

Break-Even Formulae
Break-Even Point (units) = Fixed Costs / Contribution per unit Break-Even Point (revenue) = Fixed Costs / C/S Ratio Margin of Safety (units) = Budgeted Sales – Break-Even Sales Margin of Safety (%) = Margin of Safety / Budgeted Sales × 100% Target Profit (units) = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) / Contribution per unit

3 Multi-Product Break-Even

When a firm sells multiple products in a fixed sales mix, use the weighted average C/S ratio:

Multi-Product Break-Even
Weighted Avg C/S ratio = Total Contribution / Total Revenue Break-Even Revenue = Total Fixed Costs / Weighted Avg C/S Ratio Then split by product in the budgeted mix ratio.
⚠ Exam Trap: The break-even point changes if the product mix changes — even if total revenue stays the same. This links directly to the sales mix variance topic.

Topic 04

Limiting Factor Analysis

When a resource is scarce, rank products by contribution per unit of that scarce resource — not by total contribution.

1 Identifying the Limiting Factor

A limiting factor (bottleneck) is a resource that constrains output. To identify it:

  1. Calculate the total resource required for maximum demand of each product
  2. Compare to available resource
  3. The resource where demand exceeds supply is the limiting factor
🎯 May 2025 Q3 — Twins Nig. Ltd (LP Setup)

Four resources were given: Material X, Skilled labour, Unskilled labour, Machine hours. You must calculate total usage for maximum demand, then check which is binding. In that question, skilled labour and unskilled labour were the binding constraints (used in simultaneous equations).

2 Single Limiting Factor — Ranking

Ranking by Contribution per Unit of Scarce Resource
Step 1: Calculate contribution per unit for each product Step 2: Calculate how much of the scarce resource each unit uses Step 3: Contribution per unit of scarce resource = Contribution ÷ Resource per unit Step 4: Rank highest to lowest — produce in that order up to maximum demand Example (May 2025 Q3 — Grade A vs Grade B only): Grade A: Contribution = ₦2,100 – ₦1,488 = ₦612; Skilled labour = 5 hrs Contribution per skilled labour hour = ₦612 ÷ 5 = ₦122.40 Grade B: Contribution = ₦1,500 – ₦1,044 = ₦456; Skilled labour = 2 hrs Contribution per skilled labour hour = ₦456 ÷ 2 = ₦228.00 Grade B RANKS FIRST when skilled labour is the limiting factor

Topic 05

Linear Programming (LP)

When there are TWO or more limiting factors simultaneously, you can't just rank — you need linear programming.

1 Setting Up the LP Model

Three components to define first:

  • Decision variables: What are we deciding? (e.g. A = units of Grade A, B = units of Grade B)
  • Objective function: Maximise contribution = 612A + 456B
  • Constraints: Resource limits for each factor (≤ available) plus non-negativity
May 2025 Q3 — LP Model (Twins Nig. Ltd)
Let A = units of Grade A, B = units of Grade B Objective: Maximise C = 612A + 456B Subject to: Material X: 2A + B ≤ 2,500 Skilled labour: 5A + 2B ≤ 4,800 Unskilled: 2A + 3B ≤ 3,900 Machine hours: 2A + 4B ≤ 5,000 Non-negativity: A, B ≥ 0

2 Solving by Simultaneous Equations

Identify the two binding constraints (where slack = 0 at the optimal), then solve:

May 2025 Q3 — Solving (Skilled Labour + Unskilled Labour binding)
5A + 2B = 4,800 ... (1) 2A + 3B = 3,900 ... (2) Multiply (1) × 3: 15A + 6B = 14,400 Multiply (2) × 2: 4A + 6B = 7,800 Subtract: 11A = 6,600 A = 600 Sub into (1): 5(600) + 2B = 4,800 3,000 + 2B = 4,800 2B = 1,800 → B = 900 Maximum Contribution = 612(600) + 456(900) = 367,200 + 410,400 = ₦777,600 Less Fixed Costs: (600,000) Maximum Profit: ₦177,600

3 Shadow Price (Maximum Overtime Premium)

The shadow price is how much contribution would increase if you had ONE more unit of the scarce resource. It tells you the maximum premium to pay for overtime.

May 2025 Q3(b) Logic: To find the max overtime premium for skilled labour, solve the LP with one extra skilled labour hour (4,801 hours) and find the increase in contribution. The shadow price minus the normal hourly cost = maximum overtime premium you should pay.

Budgeting & Flexible Budget Variances

Flexible budgets adjust for actual activity — so variances reflect efficiency, not just volume differences.

1 Fixed Budget vs Flexible Budget

FeatureFixed BudgetFlexible Budget
Based onOne planned level of activityActual level of activity
UsePlanningControl & performance appraisal
Variable costsSet at budgeted quantityAdjusted to actual quantity
Fixed costsAs budgetedSame (not adjusted for volume)
The key rule: You should NEVER compare actual results at actual volume to a budget at budgeted volume. You must first flex the budget to actual volume, then compare.

2 Preparing a Flexible Budget (May 2025 Q6 Style)

Egoyibo — Flexible Budget at 100,000 units
Using high-low and known rates: Direct materials: ₦80/unit × 100,000 = 8,000,000 (variable) Indirect materials: Fixed ₦320,000 + ₦8/unit = 1,120,000 (semi-var) Direct labour: ₦50/unit × 100,000 = 5,000,000 (variable) Power: Fixed ₦960,000 + ₦6/unit = 1,560,000 (semi-var) Repairs: Fixed ₦800,000 + ₦10/unit = 1,800,000 (semi-var) Supervision: ₦1,600,000 = 1,600,000 (step: ≤100k) Rent/insurance: ₦720,000 = 720,000 (fixed) TOTAL BUDGET: = 19,800,000

3 Variance Analysis

Basic Variance Formula
Variance = Flexible Budget Cost – Actual Cost Favourable (F) = actual cost LOWER than flexible budget Adverse (A) = actual cost HIGHER than flexible budget Material Price Variance = (Standard price – Actual price) × Actual qty Material Usage Variance = (Standard qty – Actual qty) × Standard price Labour Rate Variance = (Standard rate – Actual rate) × Actual hours Labour Efficiency Variance = (Standard hours – Actual hours) × Standard rate

Topic 07

Sales Mix & Quantity Contribution Variances

This was Section B Question 2 in May 2025. It is a premium topic — understand both variances and their real-world interpretation.

1 Why We Split the Sales Volume Variance

The sales volume variance shows the total effect of selling more or fewer units than planned. It is split into:

  • Sales Mix Variance — effect of changing the relative proportions of products sold
  • Sales Quantity Variance — effect of selling a different total quantity

2 Calculating the Variances

Sales Mix Contribution Variance
= (Actual qty sold – Actual qty in budgeted proportions) × Standard contribution/unit Step 1: Find total actual units sold Step 2: Apportion total in BUDGETED MIX proportions (AQBP) Step 3: Compare actual units to AQBP for each product Step 4: Multiply difference by standard contribution per unit AQBP = Total Actual Units × (Budgeted units of product / Total budgeted units)
Sales Quantity Contribution Variance
= (Actual qty in budgeted proportions – Budgeted qty) × Standard contribution/unit i.e. AQBP – BQ, multiplied by standard contribution This shows the VOLUME effect only, at the BUDGETED MIX
🎯 May 2025 Q2 — Adebat Cleaning Worked Through

Budget: Complete 6,000 (60%), Mini 4,000 (40%). Actual: Complete 6,800, Mini 7,000. Total actual = 13,800 cars.

AQBP: Complete = 13,800 × 60% = 8,280 | Mini = 13,800 × 40% = 5,520

Mix Variance: Complete: (6,800 – 8,280) × ₦450 = ₦666,000 A | Mini: (7,000 – 5,520) × ₦280 = ₦414,400 F | Net: ₦251,600 Adverse

Quantity Variance: Complete: (8,280 – 6,000) × ₦450 = ₦1,026,000 F | Mini: (5,520 – 4,000) × ₦280 = ₦425,600 F | Net: ₦1,451,600 Favourable

Topic 08

Activity-Based Costing (ABC)

ABC gives a more accurate picture of product costs by linking overheads to the activities that actually drive them.

1 Traditional vs ABC

FeatureTraditional (Absorption)ABC
Overhead allocationSingle rate (e.g. per DLH)Multiple rates per activity cost pool
Cost driverVolume-based (labour hours)Activity-based (production runs, receipts, orders)
AccuracyDistorts product costs in complex environmentsMore accurate in complex, multi-product settings
Admin costLowHigh — needs activity analysis

2 ABC — Step by Step

ABC Calculation (May 2025 Q4 — Chukwumah)
Step 1: Identify activity cost pools and their cost drivers Set-up costs: ₦1,260,000 → driver: production runs Machine costs: ₦8,100,000 → driver: machine hours Goods inwards: ₦2,520,000 → driver: component receipts Packaging: ₦1,800,000 → driver: production orders Engineering: ₦1,620,000 → driver: production orders Step 2: Calculate cost per unit of each driver Cost per production run = ₦1,260,000 / (15+30+75) = ₦10,500 Cost per machine hour = ₦8,100,000 / total m/c hours Cost per receipt = ₦2,520,000 / total receipts Cost per order (pack+eng) = (₦1,800,000+₦1,620,000) / total orders Step 3: Assign to each product based on its usage of cost drivers Step 4: Add direct costs + variable overhead to get unit cost
⚠ Common Error: In traditional absorption, candidates often forget to calculate a single OAR = Total Fixed OH / Total Direct Labour Hours across all products. In ABC, each cost pool has its own rate — never mix them.

3 Why ABC is Preferred in Modern Manufacturing

  • Products with low volume but high complexity (many setups, many receipts) are correctly shown as more expensive under ABC
  • Avoids cross-subsidisation where high-volume simple products subsidise complex low-volume ones
  • Better basis for pricing decisions and product mix decisions
  • Overheads have grown as a % of total costs in modern factories — accurate allocation matters more

Topic 09

Divisional Performance: ROI & Residual Income

How do you measure whether a division is creating value? ROI and RI are the two main tools — and they can give conflicting signals.

1 Return on Investment (ROI)

ROI Formula
ROI = (Divisional Profit / Capital Employed) × 100% Capital Employed = Total assets of the division (or Net assets) Example (May 2025 Q5 — East Division): Total contribution – fixed costs = divisional profit ROI = Profit / Average Capital Employed × 100%
⚠ ROI Problem: A division with a high existing ROI may REJECT a new project with a good ROI (e.g., 20%) if it would pull down their average ROI (currently 30%). This is goal incongruence — the division acts against the company's interest.

2 Residual Income (RI)

Residual Income Formula
RI = Divisional Profit – (Cost of Capital × Capital Employed) Cost of Capital Charge = Capital Employed × Cost of Capital % Example (May 2025 Q5): Capital Employed = ₦1,000,000 Cost of capital = 12% Capital charge = ₦120,000 RI = Divisional Profit – ₦120,000 Positive RI → Division is creating value above cost of capital → ACCEPT Negative RI → Division is destroying value → REVIEW
RI vs ROI: RI solves the goal congruence problem. A division will accept any project with positive RI (profit > capital charge), which aligns with the group's desire to accept all positive NPV projects. ROI encourages rejecting profitable projects if they dilute current ROI.

3 Advantages of ROI over RI (Asked in May 2025 Q5c)

  • ROI is a percentage — allows comparison across divisions of different sizes
  • RI is an absolute number — larger divisions will always have higher RI
  • ROI is widely understood by non-financial managers
  • ROI can be compared to external benchmarks (industry ROI, WACC)
  • ROI links to shareholder returns (EPS, share price growth)

Topic 10

Throughput Accounting

A modern, JIT-aligned approach to managing bottlenecks and maximising value through the factory — tested in May 2025 Q7.

1 The Throughput Philosophy

Throughput Accounting (TA) is based on the Theory of Constraints (TOC). The core idea:

  • Every system has a bottleneck — a constraint that limits output
  • Maximise throughput through the bottleneck
  • All costs except materials are treated as fixed in the short term (Total Factory Costs)
Key definitions:
Throughput = Sales revenue – Direct material cost (NOT contribution — no labour or variable overhead deducted)
Total Factory Cost = All other costs (labour + overheads) — treated as fixed
Bottleneck = The resource with the highest utilisation rate (>100% of capacity)

2 Throughput Accounting Ratio (TA Ratio)

TA Ratio Formulae (May 2025 Q7 — Yaro Ltd)
Throughput per unit = Selling price – Material cost per unit Return per bottleneck hour = Throughput per unit / Bottleneck hours per unit Total Factory Cost Rate = Total Factory Costs / Total Bottleneck Hours Available TA Ratio = Return per bottleneck hour / Total Factory Cost per bottleneck hour TA Ratio > 1 → Product is making profit → PRODUCE TA Ratio < 1 → Product is making a loss at the bottleneck → REVIEW May 2025 Q7 Example (Machine M is bottleneck): Red panels: Throughput = ₦12,600 – ₦4,300 = ₦8,300 Return per M-hr = ₦8,300 / 1.4 hrs = ₦5,928.57 Black panels: Throughput = ₦3,800 – ₦1,160 = ₦2,640 Return per M-hr = ₦2,640 / 0.6 hrs = ₦4,400.00

3 Optimal Production Mix Under Throughput

Rank products by TA Ratio (or return per bottleneck hour). Produce in that order subject to minimum demand commitments.

🎯 May 2025 Q7 — Yaro Ltd Optimal Mix

Red panels have a higher return per Machine M hour (₦5,928.57 vs ₦4,400). Machine M available hours = 13 hrs × (90%) × 5 days × 50 weeks = 2,925 hours.

Minimum 1,000 Black panels required = 1,000 × 0.6 = 600 hours used. Remaining = 2,925 – 600 = 2,325 hours for Red panels → 2,325/1.4 = 1,660 Red panels (max demand 1,800, so limited to 1,660).

4 Ways to Increase Throughput Without Investment

Asked in May 2025 Q7(c):

  • Cross-train workers so they can operate multiple machines — reduce idle bottleneck time from absenteeism
  • Reduce maintenance downtime on bottleneck — schedule maintenance for non-production times (night, weekends)
  • Reduce lunch breaks / shift patterns on the bottleneck machine specifically
  • Eliminate quality defects going through the bottleneck (rework wastes bottleneck capacity)
  • Offload non-bottleneck work to other machines to keep bottleneck flowing
